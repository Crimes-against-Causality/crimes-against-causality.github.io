# The Detectives

Each case in this book is investigated by one of two science detectives. They share a commitment to getting the evidence right. They share little else.

---

## Wilbert Wright

**Age:** 61
**Background:** Former professor of epidemiology and research methodology. Spent two decades teaching graduate students how experiments go wrong, then spent another decade watching journalists and policymakers ignore everything he taught. Now works as an independent consultant on disputed studies, expert witness testimony, and the occasional public inquiry. Has appeared before three parliamentary committees and been cited in two court rulings. Owns more red pencils than anyone should.

**Specialty:** Causal inference, experimental design, confounding, measurement validity. Marlowe was trained in an all-too short time when causal claims required careful justification, and he has never stopped demanding that justification from everyone around him. He distrusts dashboards.

**Method:** He starts with the data appendix. Always. The headline figure is the last thing he reads. He annotates in margins, builds comparison tables by hand, and asks one question before any other: *who is missing from this sample, and why?*

**Known for saying:** *"The data never speaks for itself. If the data starts speaking to you, consider seeing a doctor."*

---

## Nora Nightingale

**Age:** 34
**Background:** Computer scientist. Started in academic machine learning research, then spent four years at a technology firm building predictive risk models before growing uncomfortable with how those models were being deployed. Returned to research with a particular interest in how algorithmic systems fail — not because the code is wrong, but because the question the code is asked to answer is the wrong one. Holds a Ph.D. in machine learning and a well-documented impatience for people who confuse model accuracy with model usefulness.

**Specialty:** Predictive modeling, machine learning, algorithmic fairness, base rate reasoning, AI systems evaluation. Nightingale understands what a confusion matrix actually means, why 99% accuracy can still be dangerous, and how a model trained on historical data quietly encodes historical inequities. She is equally fluent in the mathematics and in the consequences.

**Method:** She starts with the output and works backwards. What was the model trained to predict? On what data? For whom does it fail — and who bears the cost of those failures? She is a careful reader of technical documentation and an even more careful reader of what the documentation omits.

**Known for saying:** *"The algorithm is doing exactly what it was told. That's the problem."*
