# World Lore: Crimes Against Causality

---

## The Setting

The cases in this book take place in a world that is familiar but not quite placeable. The architecture is a mix of Victorian granite and poured concrete. People read newspapers over coffee and check feeds on devices they never fully trust. AI systems hum along in government offices and hospital corridors, doing things their operators can only partly explain. Elections happen. Courts function. Scientists publish. Most things work, after a fashion.

This is not a dystopia. It is something more unsettling: a world that mostly works, but whose institutions have learned to speak with more confidence than their evidence warrants.

---

## The Republic of Harland

The country is **the Republic of Harland**, a mid-sized English-speaking democracy with a parliament, an independent judiciary, a national broadcaster, and a civil service that is competent in patches and captured in others. Harland has roughly the population of a medium European state, a temperate climate that tends toward grey, and a political culture that prizes pragmatism over ideology — which in practice means that whoever has the most convincing-sounding statistic usually wins the argument.

Harland is not an autocracy. Opposition parties exist and sometimes win. The press is free, if imperfect. Scientific institutions are publicly funded and broadly respected. The problem is not that citizens distrust science — it is almost the opposite. They have been told so often that science produces answers that they have stopped asking what kind of answers, and under what conditions, and what the error bars look like.

**Government structure:** A parliamentary republic. The head of government is the **Prime Minister**; the head of state is a largely ceremonial **President**. Parliament has two chambers: the directly elected **Assembly** and an appointed **Council of Experts** whose influence is real but frequently overstated in press coverage.

**The two main parties:**
- **The Progress Alliance** — center-left. Governs currently. Enthusiastic about evidence-based policy, open to technology, and prone to overreaching both. Commissioned several algorithmic decision-making systems in public services without fully reading the technical documentation.
- **The National Compact** — center-right. Fiscally cautious, skeptical of what it calls "government by algorithm," but equally prone to cherry-picking statistics when in opposition.

**Smaller parties of note:**
- **The Civic Reform Party** — technocratic and minor. Loves randomized controlled trials and open data. Has three seats in the Assembly. Wilbert Wright has voted for them twice, somewhat reluctantly.
- **The People's Voice** — populist, anti-expert, feeds on cases exactly like the ones in this book. Whenever a high-profile study turns out to be wrong, their polling numbers tick upward.

**Key government bodies:**
- **The National Statistics Bureau (NSB)** — publishes official figures on health, employment, crime, and economic output. Trusted by professionals, widely misread by everyone else.
- **The Harland Health Authority (HHA)** — oversees hospitals, publishes the annual hospital performance report, and is perpetually underfunded. The annual report is where Wilbert Wright finds Appendix D.
- **The Office for Scientific Integrity (OSI)** — investigates research misconduct and replication failures. Small, independent, underpowered.
- **The Directorate for Algorithmic Oversight (DAO)** — the body tasked with regulating AI in public services. Established with fanfare, currently staffed by eight people and a website that hasn't been updated in two years. Nora Nightingale has testified before them twice. Both times, she left the building feeling tired.

## The City: Ravenport

Most cases are set in **Ravenport**, the Republic's second-largest city and its most argumentative. It sits on a broad estuary — foggy in autumn, grey in winter, briefly beautiful in summer — and has the architecture to prove that people have been building things there for a very long time and have rarely agreed on how. Victorian public buildings stand next to brutalist office blocks stand next to glass towers whose ground floors are already occupied by cheap noodle restaurants.

Ravenport is a port city, which means it has always been a city of arrivals and departures, of people passing through and people who never left. It has a financial district, a university quarter, a working harbor that is more logistics hub than romantic waterfront, and a ring of suburbs that most Ravenport residents have a theory about but have never visited. It is the kind of city that produces both the people who commission studies and the people who have to live with the conclusions.

**Neighborhoods of note:**
- **The Strand** — the old commercial center, where *The Metropolitan Ledger* has its offices and where Wilbert Wright has a consulting room on the fourth floor of a building that smells permanently of old paper.
- **Caldenveld** — the university district, home to Ravenport University and the newer Harland Institute of Technology, which are nominally rivals and in practice deeply intertwined.
- **Portside** — the working harbor district. Historically industrial, now a mixture of warehouses converted into studios and offices (including Nora Nightingale's), actual warehouses, and several bars that have no interest in being fashionable.
- **Aldenmoor** — the affluent northwestern suburbs, where hospital board members live and where the private Aldenmoor Clinic operates. Referenced occasionally; rarely visited by the detectives.
- **New Meridian** — a planned district built in the last two decades to house government offices and tech company campuses. Clean, glassy, slightly soulless. The DAO has its offices here.

## The Press

**The Metropolitan Ledger** — Ravenport's oldest daily broadsheet, founded 1889. Respected, slightly fusty, with a science desk that is good on some days and catastrophically overconfident on others. The paper that published *"City's Worst Hospital Exposed."* It has a correction policy but buries the corrections. Ed Simpson works there.

**The Harland Observer** — the national paper of record. Published from the capital, read in Ravenport by people who want to feel connected to national affairs. More careful than the Ledger, more boring. Occasionally scoops the Ledger on data stories by the simple expedient of reading the methodology section.

**The Evening Clarion** — a tabloid. Does not have a methodology section. Has a front page. Uses it.

**The Ravenport Commercial** — a business and finance daily. Covers the numbers with more precision than the general press and with rather less interest in what the numbers mean for people who don't own things.

**The Caldenveld Review** — a fortnightly magazine of ideas, politics, and science. Has published Nora Nightingale three times and is where she is best known to a non-specialist audience. Wilbert Wright once wrote them a letter correcting a statistical claim in a piece about climate models. They published it, unedited, next to a cartoon of a man shouting at a cloud.

## Science and Technology

Harland has a functioning research ecosystem — universities, journals, funding bodies, and a culture in which being cited in a study lends something credibility that being cited in a newspaper does not. Scientists are generally trusted by the public, perhaps more than they should be, and are treated by politicians as either neutral authorities (when convenient) or ideologically motivated elites (when not).

**AI in Harland** sits at an awkward moment. The technology exists and is deployed — in hospital triage tools, fire safety prediction systems, benefits eligibility algorithms, police facial recognition, and the content moderation systems that filter what Harland's citizens see online. Most of these systems were procured from private companies who provided accuracy benchmarks and very little else. Most of the officials who signed the contracts could not define a confusion matrix. Most of the citizens affected by the decisions cannot appeal them.

This is not a story of malicious intent. The people who commissioned these systems mostly believed they were making things better. They were sold a number — 96% accurate, 99.9% specific — and they did not ask the question that Nora Nightingale always asks first: *accurate at predicting what, for whom, and compared to what baseline?*

There is no singularity, no robot uprising, no grand AI conspiracy. There are procurement officers who moved too fast, vendors who oversold, regulators who were outpaced, and citizens who had no idea any of it was happening. The technology is powerful enough to cause real harm through ordinary institutional failures. That is sufficient.

## The Detectives

**Wilbert Wright** operates from the Strand. He is known in Ravenport's legal and policy circles as someone you call when a study doesn't add up — which is often. He is not a private detective in the conventional sense; he does not surveil people or find missing persons. He finds missing variables. His clients include law firms, parliamentary committees, investigative journalists, and, occasionally, the OSI. He charges reasonable rates and takes payment late.

**Nora Nightingale** operates from Portside. She consults on algorithmic systems — auditing them, stress-testing them, explaining in plain language what they actually do as opposed to what the vendor claims. She is younger than Wright, faster in certain ways, and less patient with arguments from authority. She and Wright have a professional respect that functions as friendship, though neither of them would use that word first. They disagree, productively, about whether you can understand causality without a formal model. They agree, absolutely, that most people in positions of power are operating with dangerously incomplete information.

## Recurring Characters

**Detective Chief Inspector Orla Mace** — senior investigator with the Ravenport Police Department's Major Crimes unit. Pragmatic, tired, and considerably more methodologically sophisticated than her colleagues suspect. She has a working relationship with both Wright and Nightingale, though she would describe it as a necessary inconvenience. She was the one who, years ago, convinced the RPD to pilot a facial recognition system on the strength of a vendor's brochure. She has been quietly regretting it ever since. Appears in cases involving police data, criminal justice, and algorithmic enforcement.

**Mayor Denton Crale** — the mayor of Ravenport, now in his second term. A former lawyer who speaks fluently about evidence-based policy and has a limited operational understanding of what that means. He commissions reports, holds press conferences about the reports, and occasionally reads the executive summary. Not malicious. Not rigorous. Exactly the kind of decision-maker the book is written for. He has learned not to personally call Wilbert Wright, but his staff call Wright often.

**Professor Ines Carvalho** — Chair of Epidemiology at Ravenport University and the city's most frequently quoted scientist. Careful in her research, less careful in press interviews, and entirely aware of the difference. She is the person journalists call when they need a scientist to confirm what they have already decided to publish. She and Wright have a complicated history; she supervised his doctoral work and later publicly disagreed with a report he submitted to a parliamentary committee. They remain on speaking terms. Just.

**Dr. Elias Krenn** — a statistician at the National Statistics Bureau, responsible for methodology in the health data division. Quiet, precise, and deeply unhappy about how the NSB's figures are reported in the press. He is the person who puts the caveats in the footnotes that nobody reads. He sometimes leaks those footnotes, carefully and deniably, to Wilbert Wright.

**Ed Simpson** — science reporter at *The Metropolitan Ledger*. Not the same Edward H. Simpson who described the paradox (though Wright has noted, once, that the name is appropriate). A good reporter who operates in a news environment that does not always reward good reporting. He is the person Wright calls when a correction needs to run, and the person who makes sure it actually does. He has broken three significant science stories and had none of them make the front page.

**Cecily Vane** — editor-in-chief of *The Metropolitan Ledger*. Believes in journalism and in circulation figures, and has a clear internal ranking of which one takes priority when they conflict. She is not a villain. She is a constraint.

**ARCA Systems** — the dominant AI vendor in the Harland public sector. Full name: Advanced Risk and Classification Analytics. Sells algorithmic decision-support tools to police departments, fire safety bureaus, hospital administrators, and social services agencies. Its products are not fraudulent; its marketing materials are not technically false. The gap between those two statements is where several of the cases in this book live. ARCA's public-facing representative is **Head of Government Partnerships Maximilian Sorel**, who is smooth, well-briefed, and very good at not answering the question that was actually asked.

**The Harland Institute of Technology (HIT)** — the newer of Ravenport's two universities, with a large computer science department and strong links to industry. Where Nora Nightingale did her doctoral work, and where several of the algorithms now deployed by the Ravenport city government were developed as research prototypes before anyone thought carefully about what would happen when they scaled. The Institute has a Center for Responsible AI that was set up in response to bad press and is funded partly by ARCA Systems. Nightingale sits on its advisory board under protest.

**Ravenport General Hospital** and **St. Aldenmoor Clinic** — the two private-sector medical institutions that appear in cases not involving Elysium Medical Centre or Charon Memorial Hospital. The General is large, public, and chronically short-staffed. The Clinic is small, expensive, and cited in studies with suspiciously good outcomes.

## Tone and Period

The world of Crimes Against Causality is not set in a specific year. There are smartphones and there are filing cabinets. There are AI systems and there are carbon copies. The fog that rolls in off the Ravenport estuary is the same fog that has always rolled in. The names of the fallacies are old. The data that illustrates them is new.

This ambiguity is deliberate. The mistakes in this book are not the product of any particular era. They are the product of the gap between what evidence can show and what people want it to say — a gap that has existed as long as there have been people, and evidence, and things people want.
