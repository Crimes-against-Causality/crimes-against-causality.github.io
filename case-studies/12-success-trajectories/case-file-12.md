# Peaking too early<br><br>

## **The crime scene**

When the National Statistics Bureau (NSB) was opened in the Republic of Harland six decades ago, the NSB started compiling statistics on the number of top-performing children (up to age 14) and adults (18 and above) respectively in three individual performance domains: piano, tennis, and school grades. The latter was compared to the ranking of the most societally influential adult personalities in the Republic of Harland determined by Forbes.

A team of researchers at the University of Ravenport set out to investigate whether to what extent the stars in childhood years would be the same individuals making it elite performance during adulthood using the NSB data. To this end, they tracked the achievements of 500 Harlander child prodigies into their late adulthood and obtained counterintuitive results.  

The Evening Clarion was, unexpectedly, the first to report about the outcomes - the headline of their story reading:<br><br> 

**If you're a child prodigy, brace yourself for failure!**

*New study finds that most stars in adulthood were unspectacular children*<br><br> 

The narrative was straightforward, not that you expected anything different from the Clarion. It strongly pushed the idea that a generation of anxious parents wanting the best for their children by signing them up to music and sports lessons was entirely misguided, speculating that childhood prodigies aren't left with enough freedom to identify and develop their talents. 

Wilbert Wright's expert commentary was quickly sought. He praised the richness of the data set, adding that this is exactly the type of data needed to answer such perennial questions of human ability and development. However, he expressed downright disappointment about the coverage in the Clarion, lamenting that no matter how high-quality the study is, the Clarion will never fail to misrepresent its findings. "This is only part of the picture, which we have not fully understood yet. As one can imagine, it is more complex than it seems and cannot be assumed to hold for all domains," he explained.

Is peaking too early a hindrance to later career success?<br><br>

## The interrogation

1. For a childhood prodigy, what is the likelihood of reaching elite performance status in adulthood according to Exhibit 1? 

2. Based on Exhibit 2, is the likelihood of reaching stardom in adulthood if you were a star in childhood the same irrespective of domain?

3. What is the likelihood that a star in adulthood was already a star in their childhood?

4. What needs to be true for Clarion's headline and deck to be consistent with the data?

5. Under the assumption that the headline is defensible given the evidence, is the relationship between childhood success and adulthood success causal?<br><br>




## Answers

1. This conditional probability is 50/500 = 10%. 1 in 10 childhood prodigies go on to become stars in adulthood!

2. We do not have enough information to answer this question, as this would require knowing the total number of childhood stars by domain. 

3. This questions asks for the reverse conditional probability, which requires knowing the total number of stars in adulthood, as it is computed as the number of adulthood stars who were already stars in childhood divided by the total number of adulthood stars.

4. Both the conditional probability of being an adulthood star given you were a childhood star and the conditional probability of having been a childhood star given you are an adulthood star must be low. 

5. No. Even if the headline is consistent with the data, this would still only show a correlation rather than prove a causal relationship, as the findings could also reflect regression to the mean, selection effects, or other confounding variables.<br><br>


## What went wrong

The Evening Clarion confused different conditional probabilities, i.e. it assumed that not only the probability of a childhood star becoming a star in adulthood but also the probability that an adulthood star was already a childhood star are similarly low. The latter probability however depends on how many adulthood stars there were in total, i.e. adulthood stars who were also childhood stars plus adulthood stars who were not childhood stars. For instance, if there were only 25 adulthood stars who were not childhood stars, this would imply 50 + 25 = 75 adulthood stars in total, a probability of an adulthood star having already been a star in childhood of 50/75 = 66.67%. In other words, in this case 2 out of 3 adulthood stars would have been stars in childhood already in contrast to the reporting of the Clarion. If the number of adulthood stars who were not childhood stars were 1,000 on the other hand, this probability would drop to 50/1,050 = 4.76%, suggesting that an adulthood star having also been a star in childhood would be the exception rather than the norm, which is closer to the Clarion headline.<br><br> 


## Background

A paper published in the journal *Science* by Arne Güllich and colleagues (2025) investigated to what extent early top-performing individuals tend to be same individuals demonstrating elite performance in adulthood. Among other results, they found that the two groups are rarely comprised of the same individuals and that most top achievers did not perform extraordinarily in their youth. This suggests that the two conditional probabilities alluded to in the Clarion headline would both be relatively low.<br><br>
